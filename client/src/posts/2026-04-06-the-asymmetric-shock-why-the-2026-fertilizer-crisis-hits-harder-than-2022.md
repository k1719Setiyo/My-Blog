# **Introduction: The Core Thesis**

The current conflict in Iran presents a profound challenge to global agricultural markets, driven by the disruption of fertilizer and energy supplies transiting the Strait of Hormuz. While the agricultural sector has navigated geopolitical shocks before, notably the 2022 Russia-Ukraine war, the current crisis is fundamentally different. The distinction lies not just in the geography of the disruption, but in the financial health of the farmers who must absorb the shock. 

In 2022, robust balance sheets and soaring crop prices buffered rising input costs. Today, depressed commodity prices and deteriorating financial health across North and South America means farmers lack the resilience to withstand a new supply shock. The 2022 crisis was a supply shock hitting a healthy patient. The 2026 crisis is the same class of shock hitting a patient already in the ICU.

Compounding this vulnerability is the diminished capacity of governments and the international system to intervene. In 2022, many countries still had budgetary room to cushion the blow through emergency subsidies and targeted import programs. That room has narrowed considerably. Low-and middle income country governments spent down their reserves during and after COVID-19, and the simultaneous surge in energy costs is draining the foreign exchange that might otherwise fund fertilizer subsidies or food imports. Donor countries have pulled back from humanitarian funding, and the World Food Programme, which played a critical role in the 2022 response, is woefully under-resourced: WFP’s funding fell by roughly a third between 2024 and 2025, forcing it to scale back operations across 28 of its most critical country programs.

# **The Geography of the Shock: The Strait of Hormuz Chokepoint**

When Russia invaded Ukraine in 2022, two major agricultural producers went to war, choking off both grain and fertilizer exports simultaneously. The Iran crisis is mechanically different. Iran is not a major grain exporter. The disruption runs through a single chokepoint, the Strait of Hormuz, which functions not just as the world’s oil corridor but as its fertilizer corridor. As the accompanying chart illustrates, fertilizer inputs are more concentrated through this waterway than crude oil itself: 47 percent of globally traded sulfur, 35 percent of urea, 26 percent of DAP, and 24 percent of ammonia pass through the Strait. Unlike oil, there is no strategic reserve system for nitrogen. Potash is the only major fertilizer input with minimal Gulf exposure, which is why the 2026 shock falls disproportionately on nitrogen and, through sulfur, on phosphate production.

Between 2023 and 2025, Gulf countries accounted for 36 percent of global urea exports. The region also supplies approximately 47 percent of globally traded sulfur, a critical input for phosphate fertilizer production. Without sulfur, domestic phosphate production in the United States and elsewhere cannot function, creating a compounding effect on top of the direct nitrogen disruption. This sulfur dependency creates a second-order vulnerability that extends well beyond nitrogen. The United States is the world’s largest phosphate fertilizer producer, but domestic phosphate processing requires sulfuric acid derived overwhelmingly from imported sulfur. With Gulf states supplying nearly half the global seaborne sulfur market, a sustained Hormuz disruption does not merely raise the price of imported nitrogen—it threatens to constrain domestic phosphate production at its source, turning what appears to be a nitrogen crisis into a broader NPK shortage. For farmers already rationing nitrogen, the prospect of simultaneous phosphate tightening eliminates the fallback of shifting toward phosphate-responsive cropping strategies. For U.S. farmers, this vulnerability is compounded by existing countervailing duties on phosphate imports from Morocco and Russia, which severely limit alternative sourcing. Furthermore, as global phosphate prices surge, domestic producers have a financial incentive to export rather than sell domestically, further tightening the U.S. market. U.S. farmers are effectively caught in a double squeeze: a global supply shock from Hormuz and domestic trade barriers that constrain alternatives.

The disruption has been immediate and severe. As shown in the urea price history, FOB Middle East urea surged to $726 per metric tonne in March 2026, an 84 percent increase year-on-year and approaching the 2022 peak of $925. But where the 2022 spike was preceded by a gradual run-up beginning with the EU gas crisis in 2021, the 2026 move has been sudden—a near-vertical shock from roughly $400 in late 2025. Because nitrogen fertilizer production depends on natural gas as a feedstock, the simultaneous energy price surge is compounding production costs even for suppliers outside the Gulf. Fertilizer plants in India, Algeria, and Slovakia have already reduced or halted production. China, the world’s largest urea producer, had already suspended exports to protect domestic food security prior to the Hormuz closure. Its effective withdrawal from the global export market has removed millions of tonnes of supply at precisely the moment the Gulf disruption has curtailed Middle Eastern exports. The simultaneous loss of both major supply sources is what makes the 2026 nitrogen market historically tight.

Critically, unlike oil, the fertilizer sector does not have internationally coordinated strategic reserves. G7 countries maintain no fertilizer stockpiles equivalent to their strategic petroleum reserves. Even if the Strait reopens soon, restarting production and transport could take weeks. Those are weeks that Northern Hemisphere farmers, now entering their critical spring planting window, simply do not have.

# **The 2022 Precedent: A Shock Absorbed by Profitability**

The 2022 Russia-Ukraine shock involved two major agricultural producers, leading to an almost immediate disruption in global grain supplies alongside a severe fertilizer shock as exports from Russia and Belarus were curtailed. However, the shock occurred against a backdrop of extraordinary agricultural profitability. Crop prices skyrocketed simultaneously with fertilizer prices: Hard Red Winter Wheat jumped nearly 74 percent, while corn and soybean prices rose by 34 and 26 percent, respectively. The farmers’ income statement was strong enough that the fertilizer spike, while painful, was at least partially offset by surging revenue. U.S. net farm income reached approximately $160 to $210 billion in 2022, near record levels. Illinois grain farms recorded an average net income of $506,000 per farm. This immense profitability meant farmers possessed the financial capacity to absorb the shock.

# **The 2026 Reality: A Crisis of Vulnerability**

The agricultural landscape in 2026 is starkly different. The current fertilizer shock is striking a sector grappling with sustained depressed commodity prices and declining profitability. This is the critical asymmetry: input costs are surging, but output prices remain stagnant. There is no corresponding revenue boom to cushion the blow.

## **The Deterioration of U.S. Farmer Balance Sheets**

Since the highs of 2022, farm incomes have undergone what USDA and the American Farm Bureau Federation have characterized as a “generational downturn.” By 2024, the average net income for Illinois grain farms had fallen to an estimated $30,000, a level that would have been negative without ad hoc government assistance. USDA recently slashed its 2025 net farm income estimate by $25 billion from prior projections and forecasts 2026 income at approximately $153.4 billion, roughly 24 percent below the 2022 peak.

The income collapse is accompanied by rising debt, tightening liquidity, and relentless cost pressure. Total U.S. farm sector debt is forecast to reach $624.7 billion in 2026. USDA projects per-acre production costs to increase for all nine principal row crops. Since 2020, interest expenses have risen 73 percent, fertilizer costs 47 percent, fuel and oil 25 percent, labor 48 percent, pesticides 19 percent, and maintenance 27 percent.

Per-acre returns remain negative for all nine principal row crops even after accounting for crop insurance indemnities and federal assistance programs. Per-acre losses range from roughly $42 for barley to $210 for rice, and net losses across the crop sector are estimated to exceed $50 billion over the past three crop years. A majority of farmers report using government payments primarily to pay down existing debt rather than reinvest. Many are weighing whether they can afford to plant at all.

Notably, aggregate farm income figures mask significant regional variation. Relatively high projected income levels are propped up by record cattle prices, strong livestock profitability, and unprecedented government farm program payments (forecast at $44.3 billion in 2026, a 45 percent increase from 2025). Crop-sector profitability is deeply negative in many areas. Safety net enhancements through the One Big Beautiful Bill Act are expected to take effect in October 2026, but those changes do not address the pressures farmers face today, during the very planting season when the fertilizer shock is hitting.

## **South America’s Acute Exposure: Brazil**

Brazil, the world’s largest exporter of soybeans and beef, imports 80 to 85 percent of all the fertilizers it uses. Among its top urea suppliers, only Qatar (13 percent of imports) is directly exposed to the Strait of Hormuz; Oman (16 percent) ships independently via the Arabian Sea. However, because Brazil has no meaningful domestic urea production, the global price contagion from the Hormuz closure affects the entirety of its import bill.

This supply shock is hitting Brazilian farmers during their worst financial environment in nearly two decades. Production costs in Mato Grosso more than doubled from $172 per tonne in 2020 to an estimated $375 per tonne by 2026. Margins are projected to turn negative by 2026, with farmers facing losses of approximately $40 per tonne for the 2025 to 2026 crop season. Farm bankruptcy filings surged from 534 in 2023 to an annualized 1,560 in 2025, and Banco do Brasil’s agribusiness loan defaults surged 160 percent in Q2 2025. With Brazil’s benchmark interest rate at 15 percent, farmers who leveraged up during the boom years now face compressed margins alongside hefty interest payments. Brazilian farmers have been postponing fertilizer purchases for two to three years running, a trend in place before the war added an acute price shock on top of chronic financial stress.

# **The Asymmetric Price Response**

Unlike 2022, the 2026 conflict has not triggered a commensurate rise in crop prices. While urea prices surged 28 to 50 percent in the weeks following the Strait’s closure, corn and wheat futures rose by only modest single digits, and soybean futures actually declined slightly. This is the defining asymmetry of the current crisis.

The muted crop price response reflects the nature of the shock itself. In 2022, Russia and Ukraine were major grain exporters—their war immediately removed physical supply from the market, and prices responded within days. The 2026 disruption hits inputs, not outputs. Global grain stocks, while tighter than their 2022 levels, remain adequate for current demand. No major breadbasket has lost production yet. The yield drag from reduced fertilizer application will not materialize until harvest, meaning the supply tightening is real but lagged by six to nine months. Markets are pricing what they can see today—adequate near-term grain stocks—not what is being quietly baked in: a smaller harvest across multiple breadbaskets later this year. By the time the futures curve reflects the production shortfall, the planting decisions that caused it will be irreversible.

The urea-to-corn price ratio is approaching record highs, meaning farmers must sell significantly more grain to purchase the same amount of fertilizer. For U.S. corn growers, fertilizer makes up between one-third and one-half of operating costs. When the cost of the input exceeds the value of the marginal yield it produces, the rational response is to reduce application rates below agronomically optimal levels, accepting lower yields rather than absorbing the cost.

The timing compounds the problem. In the U.S. Corn Belt, planting generally begins between late April and early May. Farmers are making fertilizer purchasing decisions right now, at the peak of the price spike. Some who were already under financial strain had been holding out for lower spring prices, only to see urea spike 30 percent when the Strait closed. While U.S. farmers typically pre-purchase 60 to 70 percent of their spring nitrogen in the fall and winter, the multi-year margin compression documented earlier pushed a significant cohort of cash-strapped producers to delay purchases this year, betting on lower spring prices. It is these financially vulnerable operations that are now fully exposed to the $700+ urea spike. As one industry expert put it, this conflict happened "at a very, very bad time."

# **Implications for Global Agriculture**

The combination of surging fertilizer prices and weakened farmer balance sheets presents a severe risk to global agricultural production in 2026 and beyond. Because farmers lack the reserves to absorb these costs, they are adapting in ways that will inevitably reduce global food supplies.

**Reduced application rates.** Farmers will apply less fertilizer, leading directly to lower crop yields. In Brazil, many are already postponing purchases or reducing application rates. In the U.S., growers are conserving the nitrogen they have on hand. The yield impact compounds into 2027 food supply, and cannot be undone once the planting window closes. Crucially, crop insurance—often assumed to be a financial backstop—generally covers yield losses resulting from weather or natural disasters, not management decisions like intentionally reducing fertilizer application to manage costs.

**Crop shifting and acreage reduction.** Producers may shift acreage from nitrogen-intensive crops like corn and wheat toward soybeans, tightening the supply of staple grains. Some specialty crops may not get planted at all. Farmers who cannot source or afford fertilizer may skip a year entirely.

**Financial insolvency and consolidation.** Highly leveraged operations may be unable to secure financing for upcoming planting seasons, accelerating bankruptcies and consolidation. This trend is already well underway in Brazil. In the U.S., the farm economy’s reliance on government assistance leaves limited capacity to absorb a new shock of this magnitude.

**Disproportionate impact on the developing world.** The sharpest consequences will fall on import-dependent regions across East and Southern Africa, South Asia, and Southeast Asia, where governments have the least fiscal capacity to cushion the blow. Many exhausted their fiscal buffers during COVID-19, and rising energy import costs are draining reserves that might otherwise fund fertilizer subsidies. The multilateral safety net has itself been hollowed out, with WFP’s funding down by roughly a third from 2024 and operations scaled back across 28 critical country programs.

# **Conclusion: The Inflation Lag and Its Consequences**

The 2026 Iran war’s impact on global agriculture operates through a fundamentally different mechanism than the 2022 shock. In 2022, prices rose across the board, but farmers were profitable enough to absorb the blow. In 2026, a chokepoint disruption is driving fertilizer prices up while crop prices remain depressed, hitting farmers whose balance sheets have been deteriorating for three consecutive years.

What makes this particularly dangerous is the lag structure. The fertilizer shock is happening now, during the narrow window when planting decisions are being made. But the consequences will not show up for months. Reduced application in April and May translates into lower yields at harvest, tighter grain stocks entering 2027, higher food prices for consumers, elevated feed costs for livestock producers, and intensified food insecurity in import-dependent nations. By the time inflationary pressure reaches the consumer, the window to prevent it has closed. The risk is not a one-time spike. It is a self-reinforcing cycle in which each season of financial stress reduces the productive capacity available for the next.

Globally, this crisis will look fundamentally different from 2022. That shock was visible and immediate: grain prices spiked within days, and the Black Sea Grain Initiative provided a release valve. Because the 2026 disruption is to inputs rather than outputs, the damage will emerge gradually: slightly smaller harvests across multiple breadbaskets, marginally tighter stock-to-use ratios, and a slow upward drift in staple food prices building through late 2026 and into 2027. The geographic reach will also be broader. Any country whose farmers rely on imported fertilizer is affected. India has already curtailed production at several fertilizer plants. Across Sub-Saharan Africa, the prospect is reduced application, lower yields, and rising food prices in countries where food already consumes the majority of household income.

Perhaps most critically, the 2022 crisis occurred when global food systems still had slack across the board. In 2026, while global grain stocks remain adequate for near-term demand, the slack within the agricultural production system itself has been largely consumed. There are no strategic fertilizer reserves to draw upon, and three years of depressed prices have exhausted the financial resilience of the producers who must buy it. Farm consolidation has reduced the number of active operations. And government support programs are already stretched thin. The world is entering this crisis with less resilience at every level, from the individual farm balance sheet to national grain reserves to the multilateral institutions that coordinate food aid. The longer the Strait of Hormuz remains disrupted, the greater the risk that a regional war becomes a prolonged global food crisis whose effects persist well beyond any eventual ceasefire.
